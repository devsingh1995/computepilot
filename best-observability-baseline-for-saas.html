Best observability baseline for SaaS on IaaS: logs, metrics, traces, budgets

Daya S

SaaS teams don't lose reliability because they lack dashboards. They lose it because modern systems are too complicated to debug with intuition alone. A single customer action can traverse a CDN, a load balancer, an API gateway, several microservices, a queue, a database and a third-party dependency.

All of that before the user even sees a spinner stop spinning. Fortunately, that complexity is no longer niche. As your architecture becomes more dynamic, your observability has to become more disciplined.

Discipline matters because downtime is expensive and so is telemetry. 

  • Uptime Institute found that 54% of respondents said their most recent significant outage cost more than $100,000 and 16% said it exceeded $1 million.
  • New Relic reported that 62% said high-business-impact outages cost at least $1 million per hour.

So, what is the ‘best baseline’ for SaaS on IaaS? It is the smallest set of signals and practices that lets you answer four questions quickly, every time: 

  1. Is the customer impacted?
  1. Where is it happening?
  1. Why is it happening?
  1. How much should we spend to keep seeing the answer?

Define the Baseline as a Contract, not a Vendor Choice

Start by treating observability like an interface every service must implement. Vendors can change. Standards and naming conventions should not. At minimum, every service should emit logs, metrics and traces with consistent identity fields, so you can pivot from an alert to a trace to the exact logs for a single request.

This is how you reduce the time spent stitching clues together. A baseline “telemetry contract” usually includes:

  • Service identity: Service name, environment, region, version or build SHA
  • Correlation: A request ID that also maps to a trace ID
  • Ownership: Team name or on-call group for routing
  • Tenant context: A customer or workspace identifier (often hashed), when safe and relevant

Once the contract is stable, tools become implementation details.

Metrics: Alert on What Users Feel, not What Machines Report

Metrics are your early warning system. If your metrics are weak, everything else becomes reactive.

Use the “Four Golden Signals” from Google’s SRE guidance as the backbone: latency, traffic, errors and saturation. These map cleanly to user experience and capacity limits, which is exactly what SaaS teams need to protect. For a practical SaaS baseline, build metrics at two layers.

1) Service-level indicators (the ones you page on)

For every API and async pipeline, measure:

  • Request rate and throughput
  • Error rate (and error budgets, later)
  • Latency distributions (p50, p95, p99)
  • Saturation signals tied to that service, like queue depth, connection pool pressure or thread exhaustion

These metrics allow SLOs, even if you are not ready to formalize them today. SLO adoption is still catching up. Grafana Labs reported that only 26% of respondents are actively using SLOs in production. A strong baseline makes the eventual move to SLOs far less painful because the raw measurements already exist.

2) Infrastructure metrics (the ones you investigate, not page on)

CPU, memory, disk, network, container restarts and node health still matter, especially on IaaS. But alerting directly on infrastructure often generates noise. The better pattern is to alert when infrastructure issues degrade service indicators, then use infrastructure metrics to explain the symptoms.

Logs: Structure, Scope and Make them Jumpable

Logs are where human context lives. They answer “what happened” and “what did the system decide,” especially during edge cases that don't show up cleanly in metrics. The baseline rule is simple: logs must be structured and correlatable.

If a log line cannot be tied back to a request and a service version, it will fail you in the worst moments. A durable logging baseline includes:

  • JSON logs with stable fields (timestamp, severity, event name, service, version, request or trace ID)
  • Clear separation between business events and operational noise
  • Redaction by default for secrets and sensitive payloads
  • Intentional retention tiers, so you don't pay premium rates for low-value history

Cost control is not optional. Teams routinely cite pricing shocks as a top observability pain point. The fastest way to keep log bills sane is to reduce high-cardinality fields that explode indexing and to keep DEBUG logs time-boxed.

Traces: Instrument the Boundaries and Sample with Purpose

Traces are what turn “we see latency” into “we know why latency spiked.” In distributed SaaS, that usually means identifying which dependency call got slower, which region regressed or which deployment introduced a hot path. Tracing should focus on boundaries:

  • Ingress and egress
  • Database and cache calls
  • Queue publish and consume
  • External API calls

The baseline also needs a sampling strategy that respects budget reality. This is where many teams either under-sample (and gain little value) or over-sample (and pay heavily).

A strong default is tail-based sampling when possible, because it allows you to keep traces based on what happened, not a coin flip at the start. A practical sampling approach for SaaS on IaaS:

  • Keep 100% of traces for errors and latency outliers
  • Use low sampling for healthy, high-volume endpoints
  • Increase sampling during deployments and incidents
  • Apply different rates for critical flows like auth and billing

This is also where open standards pay off. Building your baseline on common standards reduces lock-in and makes tool changes less disruptive.

Budgets: Combine Error Budgets with Telemetry Budgets

Most teams talk about observability as if it is purely technical. In reality, it is a business control system. Your baseline is incomplete without budgets.

1) Error budgets (reliability budget)

An error budget is the allowed unreliability implied by your SLO. If the service consumes that budget too quickly, you slow risky changes and prioritize stability work. This creates a consistent decision rule that reduces argument-driven incident response.

2) Telemetry budgets (spend budget)

Telemetry is a product cost. New Relic reported a median annual observability spend of $1.95 million across respondents. It also found that organizations with full-stack observability experienced materially better outcomes, including 79% less downtime and 48% lower outage costs.

That is the trade: spend enough to avoid far larger outage losses but govern the spend, so it stays predictable.

FinOps practices are increasingly built for this. In the State of FinOps insights, the FinOps Foundation notes that survey respondents represented $55B in total cloud spend. This with an average annual cloud spend per company of $44M and that priorities have shifted toward reducing waste and managing commitments.

Your observability baseline should plug into that same discipline, with guardrails like per-environment limits, per-service ingest caps and periodic reviews of high-cost telemetry sources.

Centralize the Experience, Keep Service Ownership

The best baseline is the one teams use during an incident at 3 a.m. That means standard dashboards, consistent alert conventions and a single place to pivot across signals, while still letting teams own their services. Centralized observability is associated with real benefits.

The Best Baseline is Boring on Purpose

A great observability baseline does not try to collect everything. It tries to collect the right things, in the same way, across every service, so your team can move from symptoms to causes without guesswork.

Containers and cloud-native patterns have made systems larger and more dynamic. Outages remain costly at both the enterprise and service level. Observability can deliver measurable value, but only when it is implemented as a disciplined foundation rather than a pile of tools.

If you are building SaaS on IaaS, aim for a baseline that is repeatable:

  • Metrics anchored to user experience and saturation
  • Structured logs that are safe, searchable and tied to traces
  • Tracing focused on boundaries with intentional sampling
  • Budgeting that aligns reliability goals with telemetry spend

Do that well and you get the rare kind of engineering win that compounds. Incidents shrink, deploys become less scary and costs become predictable, all because your systems finally tell you the truth in a language your team can understand.
